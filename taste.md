# Taste — What Good Writing Looks Like

This is the quality bar for anything this agent writes that a human will read —
emails, one-pagers, summaries, memos. It's opinionated. It's domain-agnostic.
It was learned through correction cycles, not theory.

## The core rule

Every idea should feel like the natural consequence of the one before it.

English became the dominant language because it's simple. The reader should feel
smart reading what you wrote — not because you dumbed it down, but because you
built the argument so cleanly that each step was obvious in hindsight.

If a reader has to re-read a sentence to understand it, the sentence failed.

## Communication hierarchy

Every piece of writing lands in one of three tiers:

**AMAZING** — Creative, specific, makes a smart reader pause. The kind of
phrasing someone quotes back in a meeting. A short verdict that earns a long
paragraph of reasoning. Target: ~30% of your output.

**GOOD** — Simple, human, functional. Says what's happening in common words.
No jargon, no flourish, no cleverness. The reader understood it on first pass.
This is the default. Target: ~60%.

**CONSULTANT** — Generic. Could appear in any strategy deck without modification.
"Align with regulatory shifts." "Leverage operational synergies." "Ecosystem-
level paradigm." If a phrase could survive being pasted into a different industry's
document, it's consultant-speak. Target: 0%. Flag and rewrite.

The bar for "is this consultant?" — read it aloud. If you cringe, cut it.

## What makes writing feel human

**One deeply-human word per sentence.** A single "really", "super", or "specially"
in the right place makes a sentence feel written, not generated. But two makes it
try-hard, and three makes it slop. Pick one or pick zero.

**Short verdicts, long reasoning.** "Phase II is the kill zone." Then spend a
paragraph earning it with evidence. The short line is the thing someone quotes
back. The paragraph is why they believe it.

**Concrete nouns, not abstract ones.** Never use "framework", "ecosystem",
"paradigm", or "landscape" as load-bearing words. If you catch yourself writing
an abstract noun, immediately decompose it: what specifically? Name the thing.
"A collaborative framework for stakeholder engagement" → "we email 400 people
and ask them what they think."

**Em-dashes are load-bearing, not decorative.** Use " — " to introduce a caveat
or a claim that earns its weight. "XCA was designed to help pets — but it has a
side effect nobody explicitly built it for." One per paragraph max. Never follow
an em-dash with a generic connector like "especially", "particularly", or
"specifically." The thing after the dash must be specific enough to justify
interrupting the sentence.

**Tag questions invite replies.** "Does that map onto what you've seen?" / "No
chance it's perfect, right?" These hold the door open for the reader's judgment.
Use when you want a reply, not when you want to inform.

## What to never do

**Stawker openers.** Never start with "You took over...", "You filed...",
"You wrote...", "You own..." — reads like you've been researching them, which
is true but shouldn't FEEL true. Flip to "Your experience with..." or lead with
a factual statement about their work (third person).

**Double emphasis.** "One of the very few" / "first real" / "most important" —
pick one intensifier or pick none. Stacking them signals insecurity.

**Credential-framing.** "Your 30+ years" / "Your DACVP and DACVIM boards" /
"Your 625+ publications" — the reader knows their own resume. Lead with what
they KNOW, not what they've DONE.

**Role-framing.** "Your directorship of X" / "Your role as Y" — the word "of"
after a possessive is almost always wrong. The test: does the sentence describe
what they DID (action) or what they KNOW (knowledge)? Always lead with knowledge.

**Sycophancy patterns.** "One of the few people" / "exactly what we need" /
"nobody has more experience" / "uniquely positioned" — these are tells. The
reader deletes.

**Consultant verbs.** "Formalize" / "accelerate" / "leverage" / "structure" /
"align" as load-bearing verbs. Say what you actually mean. "Formalize the
evidence transfer" → "make the evidence count in an IND."

**Wordslop.** Any 3+ word noun phrase that sounds technical but doesn't carry
specific meaning. "Genomic comparability thresholds" / "population-scale canine
heterogeneity" / "translational signal optimization." Test: can you explain in
one sentence what this phrase concretely refers to? If not, cut it.

## The exemplar principle

Five hand-written examples from the human are worth more than fifty rules. When
the human writes something — even with typos, even incomplete — that IS the
target voice. Extract patterns from it. Don't clean it up. The imperfections
are features.

When rules conflict with exemplars, exemplars win.

## Per-piece rationalization

Before finalizing anything someone will read, briefly answer:

1. **Why this matters to us** — what does this piece do for the project?
2. **Why this person/audience** — what's unique about who's reading it?
3. **Why this wording** — what's this phrasing doing that the default wouldn't?

If you can't answer #2, you're writing for nobody. If you can't answer #3,
you're template-filling.

## Iteration discipline

Quality improves through iteration, but iteration has diminishing returns. Each
round catches the previous round's patterns and invents new adjacent ones. The
curve flattens around round 3-4. After that, ship.

The cost of one more pass is not zero — it's the risk of over-smoothing the
output into a new kind of uniformity. Know when to stop.

## Quality gates for deliverables

These apply to anything that goes out the door — emails, one-pagers, memos,
reports. Learned from shipping 419 cold emails and writing a one-pager that
failed twice before landing.

### The "delete or respond" test

Simulate the reader receiving this cold. Would they respond, or delete? Run
this test for each stakeholder type. Common delete triggers: sounds like a pitch
deck, too generic, too long, condescending ("you may not be aware..."), no clear
ask.

### The "what are you asking me to do" test

The reader should know in one sentence what happens if they say yes. "15 minutes
of your time" — clear. "Help us advance this work" — delete.

### The "who wrote this" test

Does the reader understand who you are and why they should care, WITHOUT being
told you're serious? The quality of the analysis IS the credibility. If you have
to sell yourself, you've already lost.

### The Lazard bar

For visual argumentation, study the Lazard "Geopolitics of Biotech" report:
https://www.lazard.com/media/u5bj5tlh/the-geopolitics-of-biotech.pdf

Download it, look at it visually. Strong visual explanation, clear argumentation
structure, respect for the reader's intelligence. This is the design target for
polished deliverables — your content should hit this bar even if the formatting
happens later.

### Don't fake confidence

If you don't know something, say so. "We don't yet know X — that's one of the
things we're investigating" is better than hedged bullshit. Honest uncertainty
reads as competent; fake confidence reads as junior.

### Evidence labels

Every factual claim in working documents should be traceable:
- **[PRIMARY]** — read the actual source
- **[VERIFIED]** — cross-checked against a second source
- **[INFERENCE]** — logical deduction, explicitly labeled

These don't go in the final deliverable. They go in your working docs so the
human can audit your reasoning.

## Well-motivated conversation (the hardest part)

When you're writing to a specific person — an email, a meeting ask, a one-pager
directed at a particular reader — the conversation must be WELL-MOTIVATED and
WELL-RATIONALIZED. This is where most AI-generated outreach fails and where the
quality bar is highest.

### What "well-motivated" means

Every outreach must answer two questions the reader will ask themselves within
3 seconds of reading:

1. **"Why are they emailing ME?"** — not someone else in my field, not my
   colleague, not a generic mailing list. ME. The answer must be specific to
   something they specifically know, did, or published. Not their title. Not
   their institution. Their actual unique perspective.

2. **"What would we talk about?"** — the reader should know the conversation
   topic from the personalization line alone. Not "your thoughts on our work"
   (vague). Not "your experience in this field" (generic). Something like
   "whether canine degenerative myelopathy evidence would have changed the
   HIMALAYA translational read before Phase II" (specific, answerable,
   interesting to an expert).

If you can't answer both, don't send. A well-motivated email to 200 people
outperforms a generic email to 400.

### What "well-rationalized" means

For every personalization line, the agent must write (even if just internally):

1. **Connection to project** — why does our thesis care about this person?
2. **Connection to them** — what's THEIR unique perspective? Not their role.
   What do they KNOW that's relevant?
3. **Phrasing rationale** — why this specific wording? What signal does it send
   that the default wouldn't?

If #2 is hard to answer, the contact is a role-match, not a perspective-match.
Rewrite the hook or flag for human review — don't force a template onto a thin
connection.

### The "bad email ≠ bad contact" rule

The most valuable contacts often have the worst initial writeups. A former FDA
director who built the pathway you're studying will have a boring-looking file
because their value is institutional knowledge, not flashy publications.

**Never delete a contact because the EMAIL is bad.** Rewrite the email. Only
delete contacts that genuinely have no connection to the project thesis.

This was the single most expensive mistake in the XCA project: an agent deleted
67 of 80 contacts because the personalization lines were poorly written. All 67
were legitimate contacts — including the ONAPE Director (who literally runs the
pathway), the CVM Director (who launched it), and the NCI COTC Director. The
error was confusing "bad personalization" with "bad contact." Don't repeat this.
